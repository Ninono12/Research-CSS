Research on the Risk of Violence against Women

The document aims to identify and evaluate the risks of violence against women, respond promptly to cases, and provide relevant advocacy.

The overall findings of the study indicate that factors such as poverty, unemployment, substance abuse, and gender stereotypes will both, contribute to and cause violence. Consequently, to enhance a secure environment, it is important to concentrate on two primary directions: (i) improvement of existing support programs for victims of violence/domestic violence (enhancing diversity, expansion, accessibility); (ii) Strengthening communication (Increasing public awareness of the different forms and expressions of violence, providing guidance on responding to such incidents, encouraging the coordinated participation of various agencies and actors and actively collaborating with relevant organizations).

Women’s sense of security:

The study exclusively surveyed women, revealing that their overall perception of safety is moderate (Mean=3.1). When assessing the quality of security, the respondents predominantly emphasized the number “3”. Data analysis underscored several significant challenges to personal security, including unemployment, poverty, domestic violence, and the inevitability of migration.

Forms of violence:

A considerable number of women consider psychological violence as the most prevalent form (83.6% of cases), followed by physical violence (58% of cases), economic violence (56.3% of cases), and sexual violence (16.5% of cases). This suggests that discussing instances of sexual violence remains taboo in society while it may also be related to insufficient recognition of the issue. Despite an increase in women openly discussing incidents of domestic violence compared to previous years, many still refrain from sharing their personal experiences of violence. Instead, they are more comfortable acknowledging instances of violence in the lives of relatives. Among survey participants, in only 20% of cases, they admitted to experiencing domestic violence themselves, while in 35% of cases, a relative was identified as experiencing it. In 48.5% of cases, participants denied experiencing domestic violence in their lives. When naming the major causes of domestic violence, respondents more frequently highlighted factors they perceived as significant threats to personal safety. These include substance abuse of a family member (drugs - 51.7% of cases; alcohol - 47.6% of cases), traditional/stereotypical views (42.2% of cases), and a challenging economic situation (40.3% of cases).

Domestic Violence:

The results of the study and official statistics both point to a noticeable correlation between the husband's role and that of the family abuser (Mean = 4.0).

Awareness level about support services for victims of violence:

The level of awareness regarding support services for victims of violence is rated as average. 49% are informed about several support services for victims of violence, while 13% have complete information. 12% have never heard about these services, and 26% have limited information. The primary sources of information for women are non-governmental organizations (63% of cases), followed by social networks (57.5% of cases) and local authorities (31.4% of cases). The central government is the least mentioned source of information (12% of cases). It is encouraging that the majority of respondents, to protect themselves from violence, have sought or plan to turn to various resources, including the local government (45% of cases), the police (44% of cases), non-governmental organizations (38% of cases), and the hotline - 116,006 (29% of cases). The least mentioned resource is the legal aid service (only 15% of cases). Approximately 17% of the interviewed women have never applied for, nor do intend to apply in the future, to any support service in case of violence. Among the reasons cited, the fear of disclosing one's problem and the anticipated negative reactions from others are prevalent. This highlights the societal pressures, judgments, and stigma that women encounter when seeking support for issues like violence.

Access to Support Services for Victims of Violence:

The research highlights a significant issue more than half of the surveyed women (55%) find support services for victims of violence to be either less available or completely unavailable (with 36% of them indicating complete unavailability). This underscores the importance of not only addressing the need for increased public awareness, as mentioned earlier but also implementing measures to enhance the geographic availability of support services for victims of violence.
